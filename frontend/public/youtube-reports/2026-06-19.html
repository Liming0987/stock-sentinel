This is part two of Parkev's deep dive into Adobe, focused specifically on how AI is reshaping the business model. He acknowledges that the surge in traffic Adobe is seeing — up 35% year-over-year — sounds positive on the surface, but highlights an important concern: that traffic is not converting into paying subscribers, suggesting that the competitive pressure from free AI tools is eroding Adobe's traditional value proposition.

Adobe's strategic response is to introduce a freemium model, removing paywalls on products like Acrobat and Express to let users experience the services before being asked to pay. Early tests showed enough success to expand the approach more broadly. Monthly active users on creator freemium grew from 50 million to 90 million year-over-year. Parkev frames this as a company still in experimentation mode — it has the cash flow and customer base to iterate, but the outcome is uncertain.

A core structural argument Parkev raises: current AI competition (LLMs from Google, OpenAI, Anthropic) is largely free today because those companies are burning billions to acquire users. That is not a sustainable equilibrium. When AI providers eventually raise prices to reach profitability, the competitive landscape for Adobe will shift again, potentially in its favor. The current fear of mass exodus from Adobe may be premature.

Despite these pressures, Adobe is posting 10–12% revenue growth while losing market share, because the total addressable market is growing. AI-first ARR has tripled year-over-year but remains small — just above $500M — versus total ARR above $25B. Parkev says he is not yet convinced the strategy is working and plans to continue the deep dive in subsequent parts.
