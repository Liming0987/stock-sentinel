Canada's Exit Tax Explained
2026-06-26 · 21m
Richard from The Plain Bagel delivers a thorough explainer on Canada's departure tax, prompted by viral social media posts from Concordia professor Gad Saad who complained about the tax burden he faces when relocating from Quebec to the United States. Richard's goal is to correct widespread misrepresentations circulating online, where commenters interpreted quoted tax rates of 55-65% as departure-specific levy rates rather than Quebec's top combined marginal income tax rate.

The core mechanism is a 'deemed disposition' — when a Canadian becomes a non-resident by severing residential ties, the government treats all non-exempt assets as though they were sold on the day before departure and taxes any unrealized capital gains. In Canada, capital gains are taxed at half the marginal income tax rate, meaning the maximum capital gains departure tax in Quebec is approximately 26.65%, not 55%. Richard explains that this is actually below the capital gains tax rate in high-tax US states like California.

Richard outlines extensive exemptions: all registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, pension plans, RESP, RDSP), Canadian real estate, most business property in permanent Canadian establishments, and assets below $10,000 in value or if total assets are under $25,000. For most Canadians, the departure tax only meaningfully applies to non-registered investment accounts holding appreciated assets — stocks, bonds, foreign real estate, artwork, jewelry over $10,000.

The most complex and costly scenario applies to Canadians who hold private corporations — potentially Saad's situation. If a private corporation becomes non-resident when the owner leaves, there can be both a deemed disposition on the shares at fair market value and an additional 25% tax on net assets leaving the corporate structure, an amount designed to recapture the tax deferral benefit the corporation previously enjoyed. Richard notes this combination can create substantial bills, particularly for businesses with low cost basis and high fair market value.

The video compares Canada's departure tax to equivalent regimes in 5 of the 7 G7 nations, the US, Australia, Netherlands, Norway, and South Korea — all of which have similar expatriation taxes. The US is unique in that its exit tax is tied to citizenship renunciation rather than residency change, because the US taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live, eliminating the tax-avoidance incentive that drives residency-based departure taxes elsewhere.

  • Registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, pension, RESP) are fully exempt, meaning most ordinary Canadians face little to no departure tax
  • The complex and costly scenario is for owners of private Canadian corporations, who may face both deemed disposition on shares and a 25% additional charge on assets within the corporation
  • Exit taxes are common internationally — 5 of 7 G7 nations plus Australia, Netherlands, Norway, and South Korea all have similar regimes
  • The US exit tax differs by being triggered on citizenship renunciation rather than residency change, because the US taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of domicile
