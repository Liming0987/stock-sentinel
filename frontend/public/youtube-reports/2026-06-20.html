Should You Buy Micron Stock Before the Huge Investor Update? | MU Stock Analysis
2026-06-20 · 14m
Parkev Tatevosian addresses a common viewer question ahead of Micron's June 24, 2026 earnings report: should you buy MU stock now or wait? He begins by recapping the company's extraordinary recent performance — Micron's stock is up over 775% in the past year — and reaffirming his long-standing buy thesis, which he first published over a year ago. The catalyst has been surging AI-driven demand from data centers at a time when industry supply remains constrained, creating a pricing environment where Micron holds significant leverage.

The recent quarterly numbers are striking. Revenue came in at $23.9 billion, nearly tripling year-over-year. Gross margins expanded to 74.4% and operating margins to 67.6% — figures Parkev notes are approaching software-company territory for a manufacturer that produces physical chips entirely in-house. Free cash flow was $7 billion after $5 billion in capital expenditure. Looking ahead, management guided Q3 revenue of $33.5 billion (a ~50% sequential jump) and gross margins climbing to an unprecedented 81%.

Despite the bullish fundamental picture, Parkev raises a structural concern: semiconductors are a cyclical industry, and current peak margins make forward multiples look deceptively cheap. At 11x forward earnings and 12x forward operating cash flow — roughly half the S&P 500 average — the stock screens inexpensively, but those multiples are built on record-high denominators. He also flags a subtle management signal: with the stock near all-time highs, the team chose to pay down $4.6 billion in debt rather than execute meaningful buybacks, suggesting insiders see the valuation as stretched.

Parkev updated his DCF model to an intrinsic value of $915 per share against a market price of $1,054, placing the stock above his margin of safety threshold. He maintains a buy rating but explicitly advises against purchasing before earnings given the heightened volatility event risk and his view that downside exceeds upside at current prices after an 800% run. His recommendation: wait for the results and the post-earnings price reaction before adding exposure.

This Chart Lines Up Eerily With 1929, Great Depression Coming?
2026-06-20 · 17m
Gareth Soloway presents a macro bear case built around a 100-year cycle comparison between the 1920s and today. He argues that multiple structural parallels — post-pandemic boom, transformative technology euphoria, retail speculation, elite information weaponization, circular capital flows among mega-caps, and extreme market concentration — closely mirror the conditions of the late 1920s ahead of the 1929 crash and Great Depression.

Soloway draws specific parallels: the Spanish flu of 1918 maps to COVID-19 in 2020 as the demand catalyst; electrification and the automobile map to AI and cloud infrastructure; bucket shops and margin speculation map to zero-day options and prediction markets; and 1920s utility holding companies recycling capital internally maps to today's mega-cap tech firms investing in AI startups that immediately route funds back to buy cloud and chips from the same parent companies.

His chart analysis centers on the Dow Jones logarithmic chart, where he identifies a long-term parallel channel. He notes the market currently sits above the upper band of this channel — the same position it was in just before the 1929 crash. Using a date-range measurement, he calculates that approximately 3,000 days elapsed from the post-Spanish flu bull market start to the 1929 top. Applying the same 3,000-day window from the COVID low in 2020 projects a potential market top around mid-to-late 2028, leading into a 2029 crash — the 100-year anniversary. He is explicit that this is speculative and markets could see further 20–30% corrections along the way before any final top.

Soloway stops short of making any specific stock recommendations. The video is a macro warning framework, not a trade idea, and he acknowledges the scenario may not materialize.

In Part 3 of his Adobe deep dive, Parkev Tatevosian focuses on leadership transitions at the company and what they mean for the freemium strategy Adobe has recently adopted. The news catalyst is the CFO's announced resignation to become CEO of Marvell, with a 20-year Adobe veteran named as interim CFO. Separately, the sitting CEO is transitioning to chairman, meaning Adobe is simultaneously searching for both a new CEO and has recently lost its CFO — an unusual situation that coincides with a beat-and-raise quarter and raised fiscal 2026 revenue and EPS guidance.

Parkev's central concern is the strategic uncertainty this creates. Adobe is mid-pivot to a freemium model designed to convert high traffic volumes into paying customers. However, management transitions at this scale typically make incoming leaders hesitant to inherit a fixed strategy they didn't design. If the board constrains the new CEO to continue the freemium path, it makes the role less attractive to strong candidates. If the new CEO gets full discretion, the current strategy may be abandoned. Either outcome introduces meaningful uncertainty.

On the underlying business, Parkev notes that Adobe is seeing impressive demand signals — website traffic grew 40% year-over-year — but the company has so far struggled to convert free users into paying subscribers. He draws an analogy to Nike's failed direct-to-consumer pivot as a cautionary example of a strategy that sounded compelling but destroyed value in practice. That said, he acknowledges Adobe's structural strengths: nearly a billion monthly active users across its suite and significant cash generation that gives management room to experiment.

Parkev does not deliver a buy or sell rating in this video, framing it as part three of an ongoing series. The tone is cautious — he is watching whether the conversion from free to paid accelerates before committing to a bullish view.
